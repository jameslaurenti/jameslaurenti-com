Beverly, Massachusetts · How the money works

A state law caps how fast Beverly can raise money.
Its costs rise faster anyway.

Every fight over City Hall, trash fees, or teaching jobs sits on top of one machine. A voter-passed 1980 law, Proposition 2½, limits how much more the city can collect in property taxes each year.↗ Its costs aren't capped, though: salaries, health care, pensions, and special education all climb faster. That gap is the story under all the others. What follows is the whole picture, in plain language.

A nonpartisan explainer. Figures from the city's own Financial Forecast reports and operating budgets, cited throughout.

01 · The scissors

The city can't raise money as fast as its bills grow.

Proposition 2½ lets Beverly raise its property-tax take by only about 2.5% a year, plus a little from new construction. The city's costs aren't held to that limit. Labor contracts, health insurance, pensions, and special education all rise faster, roughly 4–9% a year.

So picture two lines. One is what the city is permitted to collect. The other is what it costs to keep the lights on. They start together and pull apart, like a pair of scissors opening. The space between them is the deficit, and it grows on its own, every year, before anyone makes a single questionable decision.

What Beverly can collect (revenue) What it costs to operate (spending) The gap = the deficit

Both lines start equal in FY25 because state law requires a balanced budget. Left alone, the spending line outruns revenue and the gap widens every year. By FY29 spending reaches about $198.6M against $188.4M in revenue, a gap of about $10M.

Source: City of Beverly, Financial Forecast FY2025–29 (December 2024), total municipal + school revenue and expenditure projections.

02 · The gap was no surprise

They saw it coming. And it compounds.

This wasn't sprung on anyone. The city's forecasters pegged the FY2026 shortfall at about $3.1 million back in 2022, and about $3.9 million in 2024. Two different committees, two years apart, landed in the same place. The squeeze was on the calendar.

The harder truth is the shape of it. Left unaddressed, the gap doesn't hold steady; it accelerates, from roughly $3.9M to a projected $10.2M across four budget years. The city's own forecasters now describe this stretch as more severe than anything they've faced in decades, because the usual one-time fixes are gone.↗ This is a slope, not a step.

03 · The toolbox is nearly empty

For years, one-time fixes hid the gap. They're used up.

Most people miss this part. Beverly has run deficits on paper for a long time, and quietly closed them with one-time moves: money you can only spend once. Federal pandemic aid. Shifting road repairs into savings. Tapping unused tax room. Cutting equipment. None of these raise ongoing money; each just buys a year.

That's why the city says this moment is different. The easy plugs are spent. What's left are the hard tools: raise fees, or cut services.

On top of these, the city also trimmed recurring costs by restructuring the retiree prescription-drug plan and shrinking staff through attrition (Forecast, p.8). Those help, but the city hasn't published a dollar figure for them, and they're far too small to cover a multimillion-dollar gap. Each move had a genuine rationale at the time. The problem isn't any one of them. It's that they don't refill.

04 · The choices nobody voted on directly

The machine has settings. The city picked them.

This is the part that gets lost in the shouting. The squeeze is real and mostly outside the city's control. But how Beverly absorbs it reflects a handful of standing choices. There's a genuine argument behind each one, and a cost that somebody pays for each one. These aren't math errors. They're judgments about risk and about who carries the weight, and reasonable neighbors land in different places on every row.

Guessing next year's revenue

Plan optimistically, or cautiously?

What Beverly does

Deliberately underestimates revenue. Money that comes in above the guess becomes savings ("free cash"), used for buildings and projects.

The alternative

Estimate revenue accurately. Smaller deficits on paper, more room to fund services now.

The tradeoff

The caseCaution builds savings and protects the city's credit rating, so borrowing stays cheap. It also guards against an over-optimistic guess, which could force the city to claw spending back mid-year, cutting programs or laying off staff in the middle of a school year.

The costIt books a bigger paper deficit every year, and that number is what gets used to justify the cuts residents feel.

How much to keep in savings

How big a rainy-day cushion?

What Beverly does

By its own budget message, holds reserves near 19% of the operating budget (about $24M in the stabilization fund plus ~$10M in free cash), in the upper half of its own 13–23% policy target. That's on the higher side of its peers, but mid-pack, not an outlier; several comparable cities hold more (see the data in the appendix).

The alternative

Draw reserves down toward the level its peers hold and route the difference into services.

The tradeoff

The caseA big cushion rides out recessions and keeps borrowing cheap.

The costEvery dollar held above a prudent floor is a dollar not in classrooms or services this year. And the cushion is one-time money: spend it on ongoing costs and the gap returns all at once, forcing sharper cuts later.

Whether 19% is too high is itself contested; the appendix lines Beverly up against its peers.

How to pay for big buildings

Borrow, pay cash, or wait?

What Beverly does

Borrows for major projects like the City Hall renovation, preserving cash savings.↗

The alternative

Pay cash from reserves (draining the cushion), or delay or shrink the project, though a neglected building tends to cost more later, not less, showing up as bigger repairs or closed offices.

The tradeoff

The caseBorrowing spreads a building's cost over the decades that use it, instead of draining today's savings.

The costIt commits operating-budget room to loan payments for years, with interest on top.

How to close the gap

Fees, an override, or cuts?

What Beverly does

Leans on fees (such as trash and parking) plus service cuts, and has avoided asking voters to override the tax cap.↗

The alternative

A Proposition 2½ override (a permanent, voter-approved tax increase) or new revenue from large tax-exempt institutions.

The tradeoff

The caseFees are politically easier than asking voters for an override, and they avoid raising taxes on everyone.

The costA flat fee hits every household the same regardless of income, and it doesn't grow with inflation, so the same gap is back next year. (An override would be a permanent fix, but it raises everyone's taxes for good and needs a town-wide vote that can fail.)

The pattern: on every row, the setting that protects Beverly's long-term financial strength also pushes more of the near-term pain onto current services and onto flat fees. That is the real choice in front of the city, and it comes down to values and risk, not arithmetic.
05 · The argument worth having

The pressure is real, it's getting worse, and the cheap fixes are gone.

None of the first three sections is anyone's fault. A state cap holds revenue down, costs climb faster, the gap was forecast years out, and the one-time money that used to paper over it has run dry. Section four is where the actual decisions live, and every one of them trades long-term safety against services families feel right now.

Which is exactly why "City Hall versus teachers" is a distraction. They're two different pots: borrowed building money, repaid over decades, can't legally become next year's salaries without setting up a worse cliff later. Every hour spent fighting that fight is an hour not spent on the choices that move the gap.

The disagreements worth having are these:

  • How much risk to carry. Keep forecasting revenue cautiously and booking paper deficits, or forecast it straight to free up money now, accepting the danger of a mid-year shortfall?
  • How much to save, and in what form. Is ~19% the right cushion? Should more of it sit in flexible free cash instead of the stabilization fund that takes a supermajority to touch?
  • Patch, or fix. Keep closing the gap with flat fees that don't grow with inflation, or ask voters for an override that permanently raises the ceiling and everyone's taxes with it?
  • Bend the cost curve. Where can the city slow spending on things like health care, special education, or shared regional services, and how hard should it chase new tax base through development instead of squeezing the existing one?
  • Who carries the weight. Renters, homeowners, seniors, parents, and big tax-exempt institutions don't feel these choices equally. Who should?

Those are hard, legitimate disagreements about risk, fairness, and priorities, and they're worth arguing loudly. The blame game over two unrelated line items just runs out the clock while the gap keeps widening.

06 · The conversation is already happening

This isn't a lone voice in the dark.

Beverly is a town worth caring about, and this is a real problem, not an abstraction. We're already living the effects: a trash bill leaping from $100 toward $300 or more, jobs cut across city departments, even police and fire posts left unfilled. Those costs land on real households right now.

But almost all of the public energy goes to the effects and to the nearest visible cause, the size of the trash fee and the optics of a City Hall renovation, plus the question of who to blame. The forces driving the squeeze, and the narrow set of levers the city has to pull, barely come up. The loudest take, that the city is fixing up its offices while cutting your kid's teacher and reaching into your pocket, gets the structure backwards.↗

And some people are already arguing the real thing. On the council, members openly clash over whether pitting the building against the fee is even a fair comparison, and over whether the answer is cuts, fees, or an override. Residents have written sharp pieces arguing that Beverly simply under-taxes itself and needs new recurring revenue, or questioning how the city forecasts and how much it banks in reserve. The Forecast Committee itself wants to survey residents on their priorities. These are the right conversations. But they're scattered across meetings and comment threads, they rarely lay out the whole machine at once, and a few arrive already certain of the one correct fix, an override now or just cut harder, before the rest of us even have the map.

  • Patch (Beverly). Local news, plus resident-written op-eds making the structural case. Visit ↗

A note on why this exists. I didn't make this to sell you an override, to defend a fee, or to take anyone's side. I made it because I love this place, the squeeze is only going to get harder, and almost no one has laid the whole picture out in plain language. If we're going to argue, and we should, loudly, let's spend that energy on the levers that move the gap, not on deciding who to blame for two unrelated line items. Here's the map. Now let's have the real argument.
