Part 1

Where Beverly Gets Its Money

Beverly raises money three main ways: property taxes, state aid from Beacon Hill, and local fees and excise taxes. Property taxes account for about 77 cents of every dollar of city revenue, but a 1980 state law called Proposition 2.5 caps how fast they can grow. Each year, the maximum increase is 2.5% of the prior year's total, plus revenue from new construction added to the tax rolls. The city cannot raise property taxes faster than that without a special ballot vote, which Beverly has never passed. This year, all revenue sources combined generated $7.2 million more than last year. The cost pressures on that revenue totaled roughly $10 million in areas that grew.

What Proposition 2.5 means
Think of Beverly's property tax base as a number that can only increase 2.5% per year, plus whatever new buildings come onto the tax rolls. When school costs grow 5%, health insurance grows 9%, and pensions grow 5%, that 2.5% ceiling creates a gap that has to be closed some other way.

Part 2

Where the Money Goes

Beverly's biggest cost increases are driven by contracts, legal requirements, and enrollment, most of which were set before the annual budget process began. That said, not everything is locked in: the City Hall renovation project represents a policy choice with real budget implications, and the pace of school funding growth reflects ongoing decisions about how much to prioritize education each year. The parts of the budget that grew added up to roughly $10 million in new cost pressures against $7.2 million in new revenue. The $2.8 million gap is what the rest of this story is about.

Looking ahead: City Hall renovation and what it costs the budget
A $29.4M project already approved and underway

Beverly's City Hall renovation and annex reconstruction is a $29.4M project (that includes $28.4M for the work itself and $1M to temporarily house city departments during construction). The City Council formally authorized the borrowing in January 2026. Construction is scheduled to begin in September 2026, with city workers moving back in around February 2028.

Beverly is not borrowing the entire $29.4M. The city applied $1.5M that had been set aside in previous budgets for design work, and transferred $2M from a dedicated savings account that the city had been building specifically to reduce future borrowing costs. A one-time pre-payment of $525K was also included in the FY26 budget. After those reductions, Beverly needs to borrow approximately $25.4M. That borrowing is split into two loans issued at different times as construction costs come due.

The first loan ($15.4M) begins in FY27, which is this budget. Beverly's estimated annual payment on that loan is $2 million. The second loan ($10M) is not yet issued, and its payments begin in FY28 when both loans are active simultaneously.

Here is what surprises many people: the debt service line in this budget only increases by roughly $176K[3] despite the fact that City Hall's first loan payment is $2M. How is that possible? Two other city loans from earlier capital projects made their final payments in FY26 and then disappeared from the schedule entirely. A parking and land acquisition loan made a $1M final payment and was retired. A Central Fire Station loan made a final payment of $293K and was also retired. Together, those two bonds clearing freed up $1.3M in annual capacity right as City Hall stepped in. The rest of the existing loan portfolio also continues to pay down normally each year, contributing additional room. The net result is that a $2M new obligation produces only a $176K to $231K net increase in total debt payments.

This is not a coincidence. The city has managed its borrowing this way since the mid-1990s, deliberately timing new loans to enter the schedule as old ones retire. The strategy is documented in the January 2026 City Council presentation on the project. Through FY2028 and into the early 2030s, the total amount Beverly pays on all its loans combined is projected to stay near 5% of the overall budget, even as City Hall payments ramp up.

What happens beyond that is harder to project and depends heavily on decisions not yet made. A January 2026 planning document showed a scenario where several additional capital projects, including a proposed large public services building, would push debt payments significantly higher later in the decade. Those projects have not been approved. That part of the schedule represents planning, not commitment.

Questions worth thinking about

The City Hall renovation has been one of the most debated items in Beverly's recent budget history. Some of the most interesting questions are not simply "should we have done it?" but go deeper than that.

How much of the budget should go toward borrowing for capital projects? Think of it like a household deciding what share of their monthly income to put toward a mortgage or home renovation loan. Beverly has historically kept that number between roughly 5% and 7% of its annual budget. This year, City Hall fits within that range because older loans are retiring. Is that the right commitment level? Should Beverly be borrowing more aggressively to invest in infrastructure, or less?

If borrowing at that level makes sense, is City Hall the right project? This is a question about priorities, not just numbers. The building has not had a major renovation since the 1930s and has documented structural and safety issues. But Beverly has other needs too. If the city can responsibly absorb about $1.5-2M per year in new loan payments, the richer debate is whether City Hall is the highest-priority use of that capacity, or whether there is something else Beverly should be investing in first.

Part 3

The Trash Fee: A Decision About Who Pays and How Much

After accounting for all revenue and cost changes, Beverly faced a roughly $2.88 million gap to close. Of the options available, the trash fee was the one the City Council could act on immediately and on its own, without depending on the state legislature, negotiating with outside institutions, or calling a ballot vote. Three realistic options were on the table.

How sanitation is funded
Trash and recycling run as a separate city account, like a small business within city government. Revenue (user fees plus whatever the city's general fund contributes) has to equal expenses each year. For years, Beverly charged $100/year per household, which did not come close to covering the real cost. The city's general fund quietly covered the $2.48M gap. This year, the hauler contract alone jumped $1.3M. Something had to change.

Other options that were discussed
Some residents and council members have raised other ways Beverly could potentially close budget gaps: deferring the City Hall renovation, negotiating higher voluntary payments from tax-exempt institutions like hospitals, or seeking more state funding. Each of these is a legitimate point. They also each depend on something outside the immediate budget process. More state aid requires action by the state legislature. Higher institutional payments require the institutions to agree. Deferring City Hall would have meant unwinding a bond authorization already voted on and construction contracts already in motion. The trash fee was the one lever available to close part of the gap that the Council could act on directly and immediately.

What the Mayor's Original Fee Would Have Meant

One path not taken: per Patch reporting on the fee vote,[1] the mayor's prior proposal was $425 for a standard bin. At that level, fee revenue would have nearly eliminated the general fund sanitation transfer entirely. There would have been no remaining $1.4M gap from the trash program to close through cuts.

The City Council voted 7 to 2[2] for a lower fee of $300 per standard bin. That decision reduced what residents pay on their trash bills, and it shifted $1.4M of the cost into the general fund, which required $1.4M more in cuts to services elsewhere. Those cuts are listed in Part 4.

There is no simple right answer here. A higher trash fee protects services but costs residents more directly on their bills. A lower fee protects residents' bills but requires cuts to services that other residents rely on. Both groups of residents are real, and reasonable people weighed them differently. This section is here to make the tradeoff visible, not to argue for one side.

Appendix: Beverly's Annual Loan Payments, FY2026 Through FY2034

Beverly borrows money for large capital projects and repays those loans through annual payments built into the operating budget. The city's practice has been to time new borrowing so it enters the schedule as older loans retire, keeping the total annual payment roughly stable as a share of the budget. The table below shows how that plays out through the next several years, based on a planning document the city presented to the City Council in January 2026.

A note on what is and is not confirmed: FY2026 and FY2027 figures reflect actual budget appropriations. FY2028 figures reflect authorized borrowing not yet issued. Everything from FY2029 onward is a projection based on what the city was planning as of January 2026. The further out the year, the less certain the number. Several projects listed in the later years have not been voted on and may not proceed as described. In particular, a proposed $50M Public Services Building accounts for a large share of the projected increase in FY2033. That project appears as a line in a January 2026 planning presentation. As of the writing of this document, no additional details on its scope, timing, or authorization status have been located. If it does not move forward, the FY2033 figure would be substantially lower.
